More Than a Carpenter / The Life of Jesus - flip book

This flipbook (it has two books printed back-to-back with two front covers) contains The Life of Jesus on one side and More Than a Carpenter on the flip side. The Life of Jesus is a chronological journey through the life and teachings of Jesus, as portrayed in all four Gospels. This simple compilation of Bible readings is designed to introduce people to the story of Jesus. The easy-to-understand language of the New Living Translation makes the events of Jesus’ life come alive in a fresh way. A helpful index at the end allows the reader to compare the text of a standard Bible with this harmony of the Gospels. More Than a Carpenter is one of the best-selling Christian books ever and answers many of the historical questions people ask about Jesus.

The Case for Christ by Lee Strobel.

The Project: Determine if there's credible evidence that Jesus of Nazareth really is the Son of God. The Reporter: Lee Strobel, educated at Yale Law School is an award-winning former legal editor of the Chicago Tribune - with a background in atheism. The Experts: A dozen scholars with doctorates from top-flight institutions. The Story: Retracing his own spiritual journey, Strobel cross-examines the experts with tough, point-blank questions like, "How reliable is the New Testament?" and "Does evidence exist for Jesus outside the Bible?" Written like a captivating, fast paced novel. 270 pages. Limited number available for 2004.

Beyond Roots by Dwight McKissic Sr.

Does the legacy passed on to African- American Christians reach back to a time when Christ walked with us - perhaps even to the moment of creation? His exegetical and historical perspectives shed light on important issues for all Black believers. 55 pages.

Being Latino In Christ by Orlando Crespo.

Orlando Crespo has taken his own journey from Puerto Rico to an immigrant neighborhood in Springfield, Mass, and back again to his Latino roots. In this book he helps readers reflect on their own voyages of self-understanding and what it means to have a mixed heritage from the days of the original Spanish Conquest to the present.

His straightforward approach also takes him to what the Bible says about Ethnic identity, about a people who were often oppressed by more powerful cultures. He helps us see how Jesus' own humanity unfolded in the context of a people who were considered to be inferior. Thus Crespo finds both realism and hope in the good news of Jesus.
